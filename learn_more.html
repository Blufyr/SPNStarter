A few steps above a CPN

A Credit Privacy Number (CPN) is a nine-digit identifier that some companies market as an

alternative to a Social Security Number (SSN) for credit applications. However, it’s important to

understand the legal and ethical implications of using a CPN. Here’s a detailed explanation of its

uses and the associated risks:

What is a CPN?

A CPN, also known as a Credit Profile Number or Credit Protection Number, is often promoted

as a way to start a new credit file, separate from your existing credit history. It is formatted

similarly to an SSN but is not issued by any government agency1.

Uses of a CPN

1. Credit Applications: Some companies claim that you can use a CPN to apply for credit

cards, personal loans, business loans, student loans, auto loans, and home loans. The idea

is that the CPN is not tied to your SSN, so when a lender pulls the credit report associated

with the CPN, your previous credit history does not appear2.

2. Privacy Protection: It is sometimes suggested that celebrities, high-level executives, and

government officials use CPNs to protect their SSNs and personal information from being

exposed due to their high-profile status3.

Legal and Ethical Concerns

1. Legality: The use of CPNs is fraught with legal issues. The government does not issue

CPNs, and using one on a credit application can be considered fraud. Misrepresenting your

SSN or using a CPN to create a false identity is illegal and can lead to severe consequences,

including charges of identity theft14.

2. Scams: Many companies that sell CPNs are involved in fraudulent activities. They may

obtain SSNs through dubious means, often from vulnerable populations like children, senior

citizens, or prison inmates. These companies then sell these stolen SSNs as CPNs,

misleading consumers into believing they are legitimate14.

3. Credit Repair: Some people are tempted to use CPNs to hide a bad credit history or

bankruptcy. However, this is not a legitimate way to repair credit. Instead, it can lead to

more significant problems, including legal action and further damage to your credit score.
